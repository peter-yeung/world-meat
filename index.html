What if the world stopped eating meat?

Why bother? As with any progressive social movement, it’s difficult to inspire action when the results aren’t immediately apparent. But the consequences of eating meat – whether it be economic, environmental, biological or ethical – are becoming increasingly difficult to ignore.

ENVIRONMENT

Estimates vary, but it takes around seven kilos of grain to make one kilo of beef. Pigs are about four, chickens two. Each has a vast water consumption (15,415 litres/kilo of beef) and considerable CO2 emissions (27 kg/kilo of beef). A kilo of lentils? 0.9 kg of CO2. Along with not flying and driving, going vegetarian is one of the best things you can do for the earth.

“The biggest intervention people could make towards reducing their carbon footprints would not be to abandon cars, but to eat significantly less red meat,” says Professors Tim Benton of the University of Leeds. Between 1971 and 2010, worldwide production of meat tripled to around 600 billion kilograms as global population grew by 81%, meaning that we are eating a lot more meat than our grandparents.

Four-fifths of the deforestation across the Amazon rainforest could be linked to cattle ranching, according to a 2009 study. Agriculture itself is a significant driver of global warming, causing 15% of all emissions – half of which are from livestock. Glaciers are melting, lakes are drying up. The Aral Sea, which straddles the Kazakhstan-Uzbekistan border, was once the fourth largest lake in the world. But progressively arid conditions and increased irrigation have decimated the lake and the surrounding communities that depend on it. In 2014, the eastern basin dried up completely.

HEALTH

Numerous studies demonstrate vegetarians have lower incidences of heart disease, lower BMI and lower blood pressure than their meat-eating counterparts. And according to the World Cancer Research Fund eating too much meat can increase your risk of bowel cancer. They recommend that you should eat no more than 500g (cooked weight) per week of red meat to reduce this risk.















Professor Walter Willett, of the Harvard School of Public Health, heads a team that have been tracking the diets of tens of thousands of people for many years. Their findings suggest meat eating poses certain health risks. "We found that those who consumed higher amounts of red meat had a higher risk of total mortality, cardiovascular mortality and cancer mortality," he said.

“Specifically, 3.5 ounces of red meat or 1.8 ounces of processed meat (e.g. a hot dog or 2 slices of bacon) daily led to a 19% and 51% increase in diabetes risk, respectively,” says Dan Nadeau, an endocrinologist at the Mary and Dick Allen Diabetes Center in California.

Estimates suggest 34,000 deaths from cancer every year could be down to diets high in processed meat. According to a report published by JAMA Internal Medicine, eating red or processed meat can, over time, increase the risk of developing type 2 diabetes. It's certainly food for thought.

ECONOMICS

Professor Timothy Lang, Professor of Food Policy at City University London, argues: "If we stopped eating meat we would have to resuscitate and reinvest and re-skill ourselves in horticulture. And we have to do that anyway, certainly with climate change."

What you happened if you changed your diet for a year ? All of your tiny choices add up – even decreasing your meat intake by just one less burger a week can result in the same environmental benefit as taking your car off the road for 320 miles.
