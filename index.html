What if the world stopped eating meat?

Why bother? As with any progressive social movement, it’s difficult to inspire action when the results aren’t immediately apparent. But the consequences of eating meat – whether it be economic, environmental, biological or ethical – are becoming increasingly difficult to ignore.

Estimates vary, but it takes around seven kilos of grain to make one kilo of beef. Pigs are about four, chickens two. Each has a vast water consumption (15,415 litres/kilo of beef) and considerable CO2 emissions (27kg/kilo of beef). A kilo of lentils? 0.9 kg of CO2. Along with not flying and driving, going vegetarian is one of the best things you can do for the earth.

Between 1971 and 2010, worldwide production of meat tripled to around 600 billion kilograms as global population grew by 81 percent, meaning that we are eating a lot more meat than our grandparents. Researchers predict global meat production will double by 2050 to about 1.2 trillion pounds a year, putting further pressure on the environment and human health.
